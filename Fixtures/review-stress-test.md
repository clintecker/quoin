# Field Notes: The Lighthouse Automation Project

The keeper's log {~~ended~>officially ended~~}{#s1} on a Tuesday. Nobody
marked the occasion. The Coast Guard sent a form letter, the town sent
nothing, and the light itself — a third-order Fresnel lens that had turned
every night for {--nearly--}{#s2} ninety-one years — kept turning, because
the new controller board didn't know how to feel about any of it.

## Background

Automation came to the station in three waves. First the fog signal, which
{++surprisingly++}{#s3} nobody missed. Then the radio beacon. Then, last
and most contentious, the light.

The {==decision to automate==}{>>This section needs a date — when exactly?
The town records say 1987 but the Coast Guard letter says 1989.<<}{#c1} was
made far away, by people who had never climbed the hundred and twelve
steps, never cursed the brass polish, never learned the particular
{~~sound~>groan~~}{#s4} the mechanism made when a storm was still two days
out but already leaning on the glass.

### The Keeper

Aldous Finch was the last keeper. He was {--quite--}{#s5} particular about
three things: the logbook, the lens, and the order in which visitors were
allowed to touch neither.

His entries were famously terse. A hurricane in '78 got four words:
{==“Wind. Managed. Lens fine.”==}{>>I love this quote — can we verify it
against the actual logbook scan?<<}{#c2}

He kept the log for {~~thirty-one~>thirty-four~~}{#s6} years. When the
automation crew arrived, he met them at the door with coffee and a list of
{++forty-one++}{#s7} things the building did that no sensor would ever
notice.

## The Machinery

The lens floats on a bath of mercury — {--roughly--}{#s8} three hundred
pounds of it — which is why a child could turn the whole four-ton assembly
with one finger. The automation crew {~~replaced~>nearly replaced~~}{#s9}
the mercury float with a geared bearing before someone from the historical
society chained herself to the pedestal.

> The float stays. The keeper goes. That was the deal, and everyone {++quietly++}{#s10} understood it was a bad one.

The rotation mechanism is a clockwork of {==astonishing simplicity==}{>>Is
"astonishing" earning its place here, or should we let the mechanism speak
for itself?<<}{#c3} — a falling weight, a governor, and a worm gear. Wound
by hand, it runs for {~~two and a half hours~>four hours~~}{#s11} per
winding.

### Technical Inventory

## What the Sensors Missed

Finch's list survives. Some entries:

1. The third step from the top rings slightly flat when the humidity
   drops — {++a full day++}{#s15} before the barometer moves.
2. Gulls will not land on the gallery rail when a nor'easter is
   {--actually--}{#s16} coming.
3. The lens {~~hums~>sings~~}{#s17} at exactly one temperature, and that
   temperature means ice within the week.

The controller board measures none of these. It reports uptime,
{==voltage, rotation speed, and lamp hours==}{>>Do we have the actual
telemetry field names? Would be nice to print them verbatim.<<}{#c4} to a
station eighty miles inland, where nobody has ever heard the third step
ring flat.

## The Last Entry

The final logbook entry is dated the morning the power was cut over. It
reads, in full: {==“Light handed off. She doesn't know she's alone
yet.”==}{#s18}

The historical society {~~wants~>intends~~}{#s19} to publish the complete
logs. The Coast Guard has {--so far--}{#s20} declined to release the last
volume, citing a records policy that predates the lighthouse itself.

Finch moved to a house in town with no view of the water. When asked why,
he said the light knew its job and didn't need {++him++}{#s21} watching
over its shoulder. Every keeper who came before him would have understood
this to be a lie, and a kind one.
